An RFI (Request For Information) is an official, contractual document to request information from the general contractor or the engineer. Typical RFI topics include: confirming the flow calculations of a line are done using the pipe material's true inner diameter, potential conflicts between two utilities, conflicting or missing information regarding a detail or specification given by the engineer, cathodic protection, definition of As-Built, etc. RFIs may take weeks to be answered - Identify the need for RFIs sooner rather than later. Record and log RFIs, along with submitted dates, and return dates; contracts typically indicate how long the review process is allowed to take.



RFI Log Template

A material takeoff is the review and detailed analysis of the plans and specifications to identify the type and quantity of all the materials that will be needed for installation on the job, compiled into a report or table. The task involves accounting for all material that would be either installed or used by the field crew to install the system. After a takeoff is performed, the material buy-out (final vendor pricing) is completed and purchase orders (POs) are executed before materials are ordered (ideally) needs to be ordered. There are many ways to construct systems, and it often involves detailing unique project layouts; communicate with the PM, and foreman or superintendent for the optimal perform takeoffs and order materials so talk to your PM about what they would recommend.



Be sure to review the most recent approved set of plans, the relevant details (both onsite and city/governing jurisdiction), and any specifications pertinent to the project.

Review the Geotech (Soils) Report for the groundwater level (high groundwater requires a dewatering/filtration system), soil type (sandy soil will cause unstable trenches), or corrosivity (corrosive soils require cathodic protection on metal components). If any of these conditions exist, and they are not accounted for in the budget, talk with your PM and Superintendent about how to address the issues.

After the Material Take-Off is completed, submit the list of materials to the applicable suppliers/vendors with a request for material quotes. These quotes are then compared to other vendors in order to obtain the best prices (or the best service and deliverability). At this time, different material types, constructability, and material delivery timelines (lead time) should all be considered in order to get the correct materials on time and at the lowest possible price. Make note of any long lead items that may impact the project schedule.

Changes occur throughout a job whether it comes from a plan revision, RFI, or a discovery that was made out in the field. In other words, a change in condition from what was known at the time the contract was executed. When we identify what we believe to be a change in condition, we create a change estimate (CE) that encompasses the prices for our crew, equipment, subcontractors, and materials we believe it will take to perform the changes. These CE's may also include unit priced (predetermined and agreed upon in the contract prices for work items broken down by unit; e.g. linear feet (LF), each (EA), lump sum (LS)) additions or unit priced credits. This process involves evaluating the changes on the plans to determine all additions and deductions to the original contract scope, then aggregating the changes on a

CE pricing sheet,

which is submitted to the client for approval. When this approval happens, the client will issue a change order ("CO") (a document declaring the client will pay Preston if additive or a credit is due from Preston if deductive) to the contract so we are compensated (or issue a credit) for the change in project scope.



The PEs role in the process is to review the changes, price out the additions or deductions, and log the information into a CE log/ HeavyJob, and update/create the 990 cost codes in HeavyJob Manager.

This is done as in some cases obstructions or conflicts are discovered further upstream, which could necessitate a plan change. Occasionally, in an attempt to stay on schedule clients will request Preston start construction of gravity systems upstream due to site access or congestion (too many other subcontractors in the same area) issues at the low elevation point. In this event, the client must sign the Authorization to Proceed Upstream letter to protect Preston from liability if issues do arise when construction begins downstream.

The Job Hazard Analysis is a form that is created by the Foreman before each work day, which highlights all of the Safety concerns for the days scope of work, and is reviewed with the crew. On some projects, the client will request these JHAs, so the PE will create PDF copies of these reports, remove any confidential information, and send to the client.

After installing a utility, often an inspector from the governing jurisdiction will observe the installation to ensure compliance with the jurisdictions standards and specifications before it is backfilled. The PE is responsible for scheduling/ submitting the request for the inspector to come onsite. How this request is submitted varies from job to job, so it may involve contacting the inspector directly, submitting a formal request to the jurisdiction, or submitting the request through the client.

Depending on the utility and jurisdiction, certain types of tests will be required before the installation can be considered complete and the Inspector approves/signs-off.



Air and Pressure tests involve pumping either air or a liquid (such as water) respectively into the system that includes a pressure gauge, then waiting a specified period of time to look for any potential pressure drops (some tests allow varying levels of drops). If the drop is too significant, we know there is a leak somewhere in the utility that needs to be repaired.



Bacteria tests are for potable water systems and involve taking water samples of the water filled line, after the pipe has been chlorinated (to kill any bacteria or organisms in the pipe) and flushed (to remove chlorine and debris), and bringing them to a laboratory for bacteria and coliform testing. These types of tests usually have a document called a Chain of Custody (CoC) form, which records who handled the samples from the time of pulling to testing.



Flush testing involves flowing water through the system to flush out any debris that may have accumulated inside the pipes during construction. A porous bag is fitted at the end of the line(s) to catch any debris that may flush out. If debris is discovered, the inspector will require the flush to be performed again until the water runs clean.



The PE is responsible for collecting any testing or sample data and saving into the job folder for record.



Close-Out Phase

The punch-list is a list of items created by the jurisdiction, client, and superintendent that encompasses any items that need to be completed before project closeout. These might include missing fittings, unpainted hardscape or appurtenances, signage, or other finish items.



The PE is responsible for recording the list of items and ensuring a crew is scheduled to perform the fixes.

For projects that include a Backflow Preventer, a backflow certification is required, which involves scheduling a certified backflow inspector to come onsite and perform a series of tests to certify the backflow is in working order. The client will then perform their own Backflow Re-Certifications after a specified number of years.



The PE is responsible for scheduling the Certifier and collecting the certification.

If the project includes a fire suppression/fire water system (FS/FW), the governing fire department or jurisdiction will perform a Fire Final inspection. This may involve

a system Flush test

to ensure the lines are clear of debris and observing the above ground appurtenances to insure compliance with local standards (e.g. ensuring appurtenances are painted, fire signs are installed, hydrant road markers are installed (reflective blue "dots"), and chains and locks are added; see the local governing jurisdictions standards).



The PE is responsible for ordering the fire signage, coordinating the installation of the items, scheduling the final inspection, and collecting/saving the certificate of compliance.

Some project may require submitting a document specifying all of the appurtenances and materials used for the fire system installation to confirm compliance with NFPA (National Fire Protection Association) standards.

If any of the permits that were opened by Preston from this project require closeout, assemble all the necessary information or items and submit to the jurisdiction. Be sure to save a copy of the closeout receipt into the job folder.

Throughout the course of construction, the GPS department will be called out by the Foreman/PM Team to perform GPS location records of the installed utilities. The data from these shots are then assembled by the GPS department into a As-Built plan, which shows the exact location of the Preston utilities in relation to the site. These are then submitted to the client for record. In some jurisdictions, more involved As-Builts that include scaled profile drawings of the installations are required to be created and submitted by the PE.



The PE is responsible for reviewing the As-Builts to ensure all utilities are accurately represented and submitting to the client as part of the project Close Out Package.

A Warranty Letter is included in the Close Out Package, which states that Preston will cover/fix any issues related to installation of the Preston scope for a specified period of time.
